Week 8: Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR)

Summary

This week introduced Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) — an active sensor that fundamentally differs from the passive optical systems (Landsat, Sentinel-2) we have worked with throughout the module. Where optical sensors passively record reflected sunlight, SAR transmits its own microwave pulses and records the returned signal (backscatter), enabling all-weather, day-and-night imaging — a capability the lecture illustrated vividly with Sentinel-1 flood mapping during the 2022 Australian La Niña event, where SAR provided imagery while persistent cloud cover rendered optical sensors useless.

The “synthetic aperture” concept is the core engineering insight. A longer physical antenna produces a narrower beam and higher resolution, but we cannot mount a kilometre-long antenna on a satellite. Instead, SAR synthesises a long antenna by combining echoes received as the satellite moves along its flight track — each ground pixel is imaged many times from slightly different positions, and the phase differences between these returns are used to reconstruct a high-resolution image. This is analogous to echolocation in bats, as the lecture noted — emitting a signal and interpreting what bounces back.

A SAR signal carries two types of information: amplitude (backscatter) and phase. Backscatter intensity depends on three surface properties: roughness (relative to the wavelength — smooth surfaces like calm water produce specular reflection away from the sensor, while rough surfaces scatter energy back), permittivity/dielectric constant (related to moisture content — wetter surfaces are more reflective to radar), and geometric structure (the double-bounce effect between vertical walls and horizontal ground produces exceptionally strong returns from urban areas). Phase records the position of the wave on its cycle when it returns, which is critical for interferometric applications (InSAR) — comparing phase differences between two acquisitions to detect surface deformation at millimetre precision. DInSAR extends this by removing the topographic contribution using a DEM, isolating the displacement signal.

Polarisation adds further discriminatory power. Sentinel-1 operates in dual polarisation: VV (vertical transmit, vertical receive) is most sensitive to surface roughness (e.g., bare earth, water state), while VH (vertical transmit, horizontal receive) responds to volume scattering within complex three-dimensional structures like vegetation canopies, where the polarisation of the wave is rotated by multiple internal reflections (Dabboor and Brisco 2018). Double-bounce scattering from buildings is most sensitive to HH polarisation, though Sentinel-1 does not acquire HH in its standard IW mode — a limitation for urban applications.

The lecture also clarified the three data scales — an important practical distinction. SAR data can be stored as power (raw values, good for statistical analysis but poor for visualisation since bright areas dominate), amplitude (square root of power, better visual balance), or dB (10 × log₁₀ of power, good for identifying differences in dark pixels — the default in GEE). A paper that says it “used SAR data” without specifying which scale was used has left a critical methodological detail unstated. In GEE, Sentinel-1 GRD data is provided in dB scale by default; to perform calculations (e.g., ratios, means), we must convert back to power using ee.ImageCollection('COPERNICUS/S1_GRD_FLOAT') or by applying 10^(dB/10).

Application

The second half of the lecture focused on change detection with SAR, where the approaches differ fundamentally from optical change detection due to SAR’s statistical properties.

Simple image subtraction — the default approach for optical change detection — is inappropriate for SAR because SAR backscatter follows a multiplicative noise model (speckle is proportional to signal intensity), meaning difference pixels in bright areas will have inherently higher variance than in dark areas, regardless of whether change has occurred. The lecture presented a progression of alternatives. Ratio images (\(ratio = \frac{image_2}{image_1}\)) are more appropriate because division normalises the multiplicative noise. The improved ratio (\(IR = 1 - \frac{min(I_1, I_2)}{max(I_1, I_2)}\)) amplifies the change signal by ensuring changed pixels deviate further from zero. Log ratio (\(ln\frac{image_2}{image_1}\)) converts the multiplicative relationship to an additive one, and Zhuang et al. (2019) tested these approaches against reference datasets for three study areas (Berne, Ottawa, FengFeng), finding — contrary to common assumption — that the standard ratio outperformed the log ratio in several scenarios, challenging the field’s received wisdom.

However, all two-image approaches discard the temporal depth of the full image collection. The lecture proposed a more powerful framework: computing the standard deviation over time for each pixel across the entire collection. If a pixel has undergone change (e.g., a building constructed, a forest cleared, an area flooded), its backscatter values across the collection will exhibit much higher temporal variance than a stable pixel. Thresholding this variance image — and evaluating the threshold using an ROC curve against validation data — provides a principled approach to change detection that leverages SAR’s high temporal frequency (6–12 day revisit) rather than reducing it to two snapshots.

Data fusion between SAR and optical imagery represents the most comprehensive approach. Schulte to Bühne and Pettorelli (2018) identify three fusion levels. Decision-level fusion simply appends SAR bands to the optical band stack and classifies the combined dataset — the approach most directly compatible with the RF and OBIA workflows from Weeks 6–7. Object-level fusion first segments the imagery into objects (via SNIC or similar), then combines optical and SAR features per object before classification. Image-level fusion creates entirely new pixel values by integrating both data sources — for example, through Intensity-Hue-Saturation (IHS) transformation, where the intensity channel of an optical image is replaced with SAR backscatter before converting back to RGB, producing a composite that carries SAR’s structural information within an optically intuitive colour space. The lecture noted that GEE provides rgbToHsv and hsvToRgb functions that can be adapted for this purpose, though true IHS transformation requires custom implementation.

For urban monitoring in London (connecting back to the Week 4 policy analysis), SAR-optical fusion has a specific practical advantage: London’s persistent cloud cover limits the availability of cloud-free optical composites, particularly in winter. A fused Sentinel-1/Sentinel-2 workflow could provide year-round land cover monitoring independent of atmospheric conditions — directly addressing the temporal gap that would undermine an optical-only monitoring system for Policy G5 (Urban Greening Factor) compliance.

Reflection

The most significant conceptual shift this week was understanding that SAR and optical data are not interchangeable alternatives but fundamentally different physical representations of the same landscape. Optical sensors measure the interaction between sunlight and surface chemistry (molecular absorption, pigment concentration); SAR measures the interaction between transmitted microwaves and surface geometry (roughness, structure, moisture). A park in an optical image appears as a high-NDVI green patch defined by chlorophyll; the same park in a SAR image appears as a moderate-backscatter texture defined by volume scattering within the canopy. Neither is more “correct” — they encode different physical properties of the same objects.

This has direct implications for problems encountered earlier in the module. In Week 6, my pixel-based classification of London suffered from spectral confusion between building shadows and water — both have low optical reflectance. SAR would not face this confusion at all: water produces very low backscatter (specular reflection), while shadow has no effect on SAR because SAR provides its own illumination — there are no shadows in a SAR image (though there are radar shadows from geometric effects, which are a different phenomenon). A fused classification incorporating Sentinel-2 spectral bands and Sentinel-1 VV/VH backscatter would likely resolve this confusion at the physics level rather than through the NDWI workaround I employed — a more principled solution that leverages the complementary sensing modalities.

The change detection discussion raised an interesting methodological question that I haven’t fully resolved. The lecture showed that there is no consensus on the best SAR change detection approach — ratio vs. log ratio vs. improved ratio vs. temporal variance — and that the “best” method depends on the specific dataset and application context. Zhuang et al. (2019)’s finding that simple ratios can outperform log ratios contradicts a widely held assumption in the field. This suggests that the SAR change detection literature may contain methodological conventions that are not as well-founded as they appear, and that empirical comparison against reference data (via ROC curves) is essential rather than defaulting to whichever approach a previous paper used. For my own future work, the takeaway is that SAR analysis requires more careful justification of methodological choices than optical analysis, where the workflows (atmospheric correction → spectral indices → classification) are more standardised.

Finally, a practical tension: SAR imagery is visually unintuitive. A true-colour optical composite looks like a photograph; a SAR image is a grainy, speckled greyscale where cities are bright and water is black. This interpretive barrier may partly explain why SAR remains underutilised in policy contexts despite its operational advantages — planning officers who can immediately understand an NDVI map may struggle to extract meaning from a VH backscatter image. This reinforces the argument from Week 4 that derived products (urban footprint maps, flood extent polygons, change detection alerts) are more likely to achieve policy uptake than raw sensor imagery, regardless of how technically superior the underlying data source is.

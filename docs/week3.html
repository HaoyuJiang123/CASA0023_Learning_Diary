Week 3 Learning Diary: Corrections and Enhancements

Summary

This week’s lecture opened with a useful framing: nearly all Earth observation data now comes as Analysis Ready Data (ARD), meaning the corrections covered this week have largely been automated in operational pipelines. The reason to understand them anyway is that loading a Landsat image in Google Earth Engine still requires choosing between Level-1 DN, Level-2 surface reflectance, and Level-3 science products — and that choice has real consequences for what the values in your raster actually mean. The lecturer’s point that some researchers make this choice without understanding it was a sharp reminder that familiarity with a tool is not the same as understanding the data it produces.

The corrections follow a fixed order: geometric → atmospheric → orthorectification. Geometric correction aligns an image to a known coordinate reference system by identifying Ground Control Points (GCPs) at stable features — roads, boundaries, buildings — fitting a regression between their positions in the distorted image and a reference dataset, and warping the image accordingly. The reason backward mapping is preferred over forward mapping is worth noting: forward mapping can produce predicted positions that fall between grid cells, creating output pixels with no assignable value, whereas backward mapping guarantees a value for every output pixel. After warping, resampling — nearest neighbour, bilinear, or cubic convolution — snaps the transformed grid back to integer positions.

Atmospheric correction addresses the contamination introduced by path radiance (EMR scattered into the sensor before touching the surface) and the adjacency effect (radiance bleeding from neighbouring pixels). Dark Object Subtraction (DOS) is the simplest relative approach: identify the darkest pixel in each band, assume it represents a surface with near-zero true reflectance, and subtract its value from all other pixels. The underlying logic — that any signal from a truly black surface must be atmospheric — is reasonable but approximate, which is why DOS is considered a lower bound rather than a rigorous correction (Jensen 2015). The more sophisticated COST variant incorporates the cosine of the solar zenith angle (\(\tau_z\)), while Pseudo-Invariant Features (PIFs) offer a more principled relative method, using regression across stable surfaces to normalise multiple images to a single reference date.

DN is a dimensionless, sensor-specific integer with no physical meaning beyond efficient storage. Radiance, obtained via \(L_\lambda = Bias + Gain \times DN\) using lab-calibrated coefficients, gives the quantity in meaningful units (W·m⁻²·sr⁻¹·μm⁻¹). Surface reflectance then removes the effects of both illumination conditions and the atmosphere, leaving a value that is a property of the surface itself — and therefore comparable across acquisitions. TOA reflectance occupies an intermediate position: illumination effects removed, but atmosphere still present. Some papers use TOA on the grounds that scenes were clear enough that residual atmospheric effects were negligible — a judgement call that should at minimum be stated explicitly.

Application

The practical used Landsat 8 and 9 imagery over Cape Town, which requires at least two tiles to cover the metropolitan area — a direct consequence of Landsat’s Worldwide Reference System (WRS-2) grid not aligning with administrative boundaries. The tiles were loaded and mosaicked using terra::mosaic():

Code 
library(terra)
library(fs)

listlandsat_8 <- dir_info(here::here("prac_3", "Landsat", "Lsat8")) %>%
  dplyr::filter(str_detect(path, "[B123456790].TIF")) %>%
  dplyr::select(path) %>% pull() %>% as.character() %>%
  terra::rast()

listlandsat_9 <- dir_info(here::here("prac_3", "Landsat", "Lsat9")) %>%
  dplyr::filter(str_detect(path, "[1B23456790].TIF")) %>%
  dplyr::select(path) %>% pull() %>% as.character() %>%
  terra::rast()

m1 <- terra::mosaic(listlandsat_8, listlandsat_9, fun="mean")

Taking the mean in the overlap zone is a practical compromise, but it papers over a real issue: even as ARD products, tiles acquired on different dates carry different atmospheric states, and their surface reflectance values may not be perfectly consistent over identical surface features. The mean averages the inconsistency rather than resolving it. Schulte to Bühne and Pettorelli (2018) discuss the broader challenge of integrating multi-source imagery, noting that even after atmospheric correction, residual systematic differences between acquisitions can propagate into derived products — a point that becomes critical for change detection applications where the signal of interest may be smaller than the inter-scene noise.

NDVI was then computed and thresholded to isolate vegetated areas:

Code 
m1_NDVI <- (m1_clip$LC08_L2SP_175083_20220501_20220504_02_T1_SR_B5 - 
            m1_clip$LC08_L2SP_175083_20220501_20220504_02_T1_SR_B4) /
           (m1_clip$LC08_L2SP_175083_20220501_20220504_02_T1_SR_B5 + 
            m1_clip$LC08_L2SP_175083_20220501_20220504_02_T1_SR_B4)

veg <- m1_NDVI %>%
  terra::classify(., cbind(-Inf, 0.2, NA))

The 0.2 threshold is widely used but context-dependent. For Cape Town’s fynbos — a low-growing shrubland that is the city’s dominant natural vegetation type — values between 0.1 and 0.2 are common, particularly in drier months. A blanket 0.2 cut-off would exclude a significant portion of ecologically meaningful vegetation, which matters if the downstream application is green space access or heat stress mapping. This highlights a broader point: spectral indices are not neutral transforms — the choice of threshold encodes assumptions about what counts as “vegetation” that may not hold across biomes.

Texture analysis used GLCMTextures on Band 4 with a 7×7 window to compute GLCM homogeneity. A critical preprocessing step is rescaling the data — Landsat Level-2 products store reflectance as scaled integers, and texture metrics computed on raw integers would be physically meaningless:

Code 
library(GLCMTextures)

scale <- (m1_clip * 0.0000275) + -0.2

textures1 <- glcm_textures(
  scale$LC08_L2SP_175083_20220501_20220504_02_T1_SR_B4,
  w = c(7, 7),
  n_levels = 4,
  quant_method = "range",
  shift = list(c(1,0), c(1,1), c(0,1), c(-1,1)),
  metrics = "glcm_homogeneity")

Homogeneity captures information that spectral band values alone cannot: two areas with identical mean NIR reflectance may look completely different in texture if one is a uniform park canopy and the other is a fragmented mix of trees and rooftops. Lu et al. (2012) demonstrated this in Brazilian Amazon land cover classification, where adding GLCM texture features substantially improved discrimination between forest types with near-identical spectral signatures — a finding directly relevant to urban contexts where built form diversity is high but spectral contrast is low.

The texture band was then fused with spectral data and reduced via PCA, with centering and scaling applied to prevent variables with larger numerical ranges from dominating the components:

Code 
raster_and_texture <- c(m1_clip, textures1)

pca <- prcomp(as.data.frame(raster_and_texture, na.rm=TRUE),
              center=TRUE, scale=TRUE)

x <- predict(raster_and_texture, pca)

PCA takes advantage of multicollinearity between bands — a problem in regression but an asset here — to produce uncorrelated components that concentrate information. The practical benefit is dimensionality reduction: rather than feeding 8+ correlated spectral and texture bands into a classifier, the first 3–4 PCA components often capture >95% of the variance in a fraction of the computational cost.

Reflection

The most useful shift this week was in how I think about what “using satellite data” actually means. Before, I would have treated loading a Landsat image as a neutral act — the data is the data. Now I see there is a real difference between a researcher who can explain what collection, level, and processing model their data went through and one who cannot. That difference probably doesn’t matter for a single-date, single-sensor classification, but it matters enormously for anything involving long time series or cross-sensor comparison — which is most of the research questions this module is pointing towards.

On texture: the GLCM homogeneity output is essentially a spatial derivative — it responds to edges and transitions rather than mean values. For urban analysis, this means building outlines, road margins, and land cover boundaries all become detectable even when the spectral values on either side are similar. I found this genuinely useful to think through, and it suggests that for fine-grained urban classification, texture in SWIR or NIR is probably not optional — it is a necessary complement to spectral information.

One question I have not resolved: the practical used a 7×7 window on 30 m Landsat, which covers a 210 m ground footprint per texture pixel. At that scale, individual buildings and street trees are subsumed within a single kernel. For 10 m Sentinel-2 the same window would cover 70 m — much closer to the scale of urban features I would want to detect. I am not clear whether there is a principled method for choosing window size relative to resolution and target feature scale, or whether it is largely empirical. Hall-Beyer (2017) notes that window size should approximate the spatial extent of the texture pattern of interest, but in practice most studies appear to use 3×3, 5×5, or 7×7 without rigorous justification. This seems like a parameter choice that could meaningfully affect classification accuracy in dense urban environments, and something worth investigating systematically when we move to Google Earth Engine later in the module.
